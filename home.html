Letter to the Women of Malolos

By José Rizal

Introduction

Women of Malolos

The image sketch depicts 10 out of 20 women from Malolos who petitioned for a night school.

Letter to the Women of Malolos is a historically significant piece written by Dr. José Rizal in February 1889. This letter was addressed to the courageous women of Malolos, Bulacan, who bravely petitioned Governor-General Valeriano Weyler for the right to open a night school where they could learn Spanish. Their request was initially denied, but their persistence and determination eventually led to success.

Rizal’s letter serves as a powerful encouragement for women to seek education and empowerment despite societal constraints. He highlights the importance of knowledge in shaping a stronger and more progressive nation. This letter is regarded as an early call for gender equality in the Philippines, urging women to break free from traditional limitations and take part in intellectual and social advancement.

Biography of José Rizal

José Protasio Rizal Mercado y Alonso Realonda was a Filipino nationalist, writer, scientiest, and polymath who played a key role in the Philippine nationalist movement. Born in Calamba, Laguna, Rizal was the seventh of eleven children in a well-to-do family. His parents, Francisco Mercado and Teodora Alonso, were well-educated and instilled in him a love for learning.

Young José Rizal

(June 19, 1861 – December 30, 1896)

Rizal was a brilliant student, excelling in various fields such as literature, medicine, and the arts. He pursued higher education in Manila, where he attended the Ateneo Municipal de Manila and later the University of Santo Tomas. Dissatisfied with the educational system in the Philippines, he traveled to Europe to continue his studies. He earned a degree in Medicine from the Universidad Central de Madrid and furthered his studies in Paris and Heidelberg, specializing in ophthalmology.

Rizal's time in Europe exposed him to the liberal and progressive ideas of the time, which greatly influenced his views on social and political reforms. He became an active member of the Propaganda Movement, a group of Filipino expatriates advocating for peaceful reforms in the Philippines. Through his writings, Rizal sought to awaken the Filipino consciousness and inspire his countrymen to strive for independence and social justice.

Propaganda Movement

(1861-1896)

His novels, Noli Me Tangere and El Filibusterismo, are considered his most significant works. These novels exposed the injustices and abuses of the Spanish colonial government and the powerful friars, sparking a sense of nationalism among Filipinos. Noli Me Tangere tells the story of Crisostomo Ibarra, a young Filipino who returns to the Philippines after studying in Europe, only to find his country oppressed by the Spanish authorities. El Filibusterismo, a sequel to Noli Me Tangere, continues the story of Ibarra, now known as Simoun, who seeks revenge against the Spanish regime.

Despite his advocacy for peaceful reform, Rizal's writings and activities were seen as a threat by the Spanish authorities. He was arrested, tried for sedition, and executed by firing squad on December 30, 1896, in Bagumbayan (now Luneta Park) in Manila. His execution ignited the Philippine Revolution against Spanish rule, and he became a martyr and a national hero of the Philippines.

Rizal's legacy continues to inspire Filipinos to this day. His life and works are a testament to his unwavering dedication to his country and his belief in the power of education and peaceful reform.
